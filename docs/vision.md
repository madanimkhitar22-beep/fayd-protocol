## 🌍 The Context

We are living through a transformative moment in computing history. Artificial intelligence is reshaping every domain of human knowledge, from scientific discovery to creative expression. This transformation is powered by computation.

However, the infrastructure that powers this revolution is becoming increasingly concentrated:

- A handful of cloud providers control the majority of compute resources.
- GPU shortages create artificial scarcity and delay innovation.
- Costs are prohibitive for independent researchers, students, and startups.
- Geographic and economic barriers lock out entire regions from participation.

Meanwhile, billions of capable devices worldwide — phones, laptops, tablets, IoT devices — sit idle for most of their existence. This is not a scarcity problem; it is a **distribution and coordination problem**.

---

## ⚠️ The Problem

### 1. Centralization of Compute

| Aspect | Current Reality |
|--------|-----------------|
| Control | Concentrated in few corporations |
| Access | Gated by geography, wealth, and approval |
| Resilience | Single points of failure and censorship |
| Innovation | Bottlenecked by infrastructure costs |

Centralization creates systemic risk: when infrastructure is controlled by a few, the future of AI is shaped by their priorities, not by the collective needs of humanity.

### 2. Waste of Idle Resources

- The average smartphone is idle over 90% of the time.
- Laptops and desktops operate at minimal utilization outside active use.- This idle capacity represents an enormous untapped resource.
- Building new data centers while ignoring existing capacity is inefficient and unsustainable.

### 3. Access Inequality

| Group | Barrier |
|-------|---------|
| Researchers in developing regions | No access to cloud credits or GPUs |
| Independent developers | Cannot afford training costs |
| Students | Locked out of hands-on AI experimentation |
| Startups | Priced out before proving value |

This inequality stifles innovation and perpetuates a digital divide in the AI era.

### 4. Environmental Impact

- Data centers consume massive amounts of energy and water.
- Manufacturing new hardware has significant carbon footprint.
- Utilizing existing devices reduces the need for new infrastructure.
- Sustainability must be a design requirement, not an afterthought.

## 🌊 Our Vision

We envision a world where compute infrastructure is:

### Accessible to All

> A researcher in Morocco, a student in Indonesia, a developer in Kenya — all can access the compute they need without barriers.

Compute becomes a utility, like electricity or internet, available to anyone who needs it.

### Distributed by Design

> Infrastructure is resilient, censorship-resistant, and community-owned.

No single entity controls the network. Power is distributed across millions of devices worldwide.

### Efficient and Sustainable

> Idle resources are utilized before new capacity is built.

We optimize what exists, reducing waste and environmental impact.

### Verifiable and Trustworthy

> Trust is mathematical, not institutional.

Every computation can be independently verified. The protocol guarantees integrity without requiring blind trust.
### Privacy-Preserving

> Contributors and consumers retain control over their data.

Computation happens without compromising privacy or sovereignty.

## 🧭 Guiding Principles

These principles guide every decision we make:

### 1. Infrastructure First

Fayd is infrastructure protocol, not an application. We build the foundation others can build upon. Our success is measured by what the community builds on top of Fayd.

### 2. Technical Excellence

We prioritize correctness, security, and efficiency over speed of delivery. Every line of code matters. We review, test, and verify rigorously.

### 3. Simplicity

Complexity is the enemy of security and adoption. We build minimal, understandable systems. Each component does one thing well.

### 4. Inclusivity

The protocol must work on a $100 phone as well as a high-end server. Accessibility is a design requirement, not an optional feature.

### 5. Long-Term Thinking

We build for decades, not quarters. Decisions are made with sustainability, maintainability, and community health in mind.

### 6. Transparency

All protocol behavior is documented, auditable, and predictable. No hidden mechanisms, no opaque execution.

### 7. Neutrality

The protocol serves all use cases equally. We do not discriminate based on geography, identity, or application domain.
